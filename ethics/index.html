Editorial standards.

The standards that govern every piece of coverage on ContentLore. Sources and attribution. Right-of-reply. Independence. Corrections. Conflicts of interest. AI disclosure. Harm reduction. Published in full so readers, subjects of coverage, and this desk itself can reference a single standing document rather than improvised judgement.

Last updated · 19 April 2026 Version · 1.0 Review cadence · Quarterly

These standards are not aspirational. They are enforced. Every piece published on ContentLore is written and edited against this document, and any piece where the desk fails to meet the standards set out here is subject to correction through the Ledger. The standards exist because accountability journalism — the Tebex Audit, scene coverage that names individuals or servers, financial and commercial reporting — is only credible when the standards governing it are public, specific, and consistent. Vague commitments to "integrity" or "accuracy" without specific rules behind them are not standards; they are marketing copy.

Sources and attribution

Every factual claim in a ContentLore piece is sourced. Where the source is a publicly available document, link, or public statement, it is cited in the sources block at the foot of the piece. Where the source is scene knowledge — something known to this desk but not publicly documented — that attribution is explicit in the piece itself, usually with the phrase scene-knowledge override or this desk understands, so readers can distinguish between publicly-verifiable fact and scene-verified claim.

Where a source speaks to this desk on condition of anonymity, that anonymity is honoured. Where relevant, the piece notes that a given claim is based on anonymous sourcing and characterises the source category — a server operator, a creator close to the situation — without identifying the individual. Anonymous sourcing is used sparingly and only where the claim could not be reported without it.

The paraphrasing rule

ContentLore paraphrases rather than quoting. Direct quotes are reserved for statements where the specific wording is material — for example, legal statements, public policy announcements, or contested claims where the exact phrasing is itself the subject of reporting. Where quotes are used, they are brief, attributed, and sourced to the original publication or statement.

This is both an editorial and a copyright posture. Editorially, paraphrasing forces clearer thinking: a claim restated in a piece's own language has been understood, whereas a claim quoted verbatim has only been copied. From a copyright perspective, extensive quotation of sources — even with citation — is a weaker legal position than substantive paraphrase with attribution.

Right-of-reply

Any piece of ContentLore coverage that names a person, server, or organisation and makes a claim about their behaviour carries right-of-reply obligations. In practice this means:

The right-of-reply commitment

If a ContentLore piece makes a specific factual claim about an identifiable person, server, or organisation — beyond observation of public facts — that subject is contacted in advance of publication, shown the specific claim being made, given access to the evidence supporting it, and offered the opportunity to respond. The response, where substantive, is published alongside the piece. Where the response changes the editorial reading, the piece is revised or withheld.

Right-of-reply is not a courtesy; it is a professional obligation, both ethically and legally. Under the UK Defamation Act 2013, the serious harm threshold for defamation claims applies to published statements that damage a subject's reputation, and meeting the threshold for responsible journalism requires, among other things, offering subjects reasonable opportunity to respond before publication. Every accountability piece on this publication is built to survive that test.

The window for right-of-reply varies with the urgency of the piece. For time-sensitive breaking coverage, it may be as short as twenty-four hours. For audit series like the Tebex Audit, it is a minimum of ten days before publication. Subjects who do not respond within the window are recorded as offered reply, no response on the published piece.

Independence and conflicts of interest

ContentLore is independent from every entity it covers. This is a structural commitment, not a discretionary one, and it is operationalised through specific rules.

No payment from covered entities. ContentLore does not accept payment, sponsorship, advertising revenue, affiliate commissions, free products, paid access, or any other financial relationship from any person, server, or organisation that is a subject of coverage. This applies to creators featured in Rising, servers tracked in the Scoreboard, brands mentioned in Signals, and every other category of covered entity.

No access relationships that shape coverage. Where this desk has working access to a scene source — a server operator who shares context, a creator who trusts the desk with inside information — that access does not convert to editorial protection. A source who provides context for one piece is still subject to factual coverage in a subsequent piece, on the same terms as any other subject.

Personal conflicts are disclosed. Where this desk has a personal relationship, historical or current, with a subject of coverage — friendship, prior professional collaboration, shared ownership of a project, family connection, or any other material tie — that relationship is disclosed on pieces where it is relevant. Where the relationship is material enough that coverage cannot be handled on standard editorial terms, the piece is either handled by a separate contributor (where one exists) or deferred until an independent editorial process can be arranged.

The Unique RP disclosure

One specific conflict is worth stating explicitly here as a template for how this publication handles material COIs. This desk's editor is part of the inner circle of Unique RP, a UK FiveM server. That relationship is material to any coverage of Unique RP, of its ownership, of its creator figures, or of adjacent scene dynamics.

Until an independent external moderator is in place for Unique-RP-adjacent coverage, this publication's default posture is light-touch public coverage of those subjects. In practice this means: individuals within the Unique RP inner circle are not profiled in public editorial while the COI is unresolved; Unique RP's Scoreboard entry is maintained with standard factual observations only, without qualitative commentary that would normally accompany Scoreboard entries; and any piece requiring substantive coverage of Unique RP or its ownership is held until the independent review process is in place. This disclosure is logged in the Ledger and will be updated when the moderator arrangement is established.

Corrections and updates

Every factual error in a published piece is corrected. The correction appears both on the piece itself — at the top, clearly marked as a correction — and in the permanent log on the Ledger. The distinction between a correction (a factual error being fixed) and an update (new information being added to an evolving story) is maintained carefully; corrections imply the piece was wrong, updates imply the situation has changed.

A correction is triggered when a reader, a subject of coverage, or this desk itself identifies a factual claim in a published piece that is demonstrably incorrect. The standard for demonstrably is evidence — a source document, a screenshot, a public statement — not argument. A reader who disagrees with a ContentLore interpretation has not identified a correction; a reader who identifies a factual error has.

Corrections are made promptly. The target is within twenty-four hours of confirmed identification, allowing for the time needed to verify the correction itself. Once the correction is made, the Ledger entry is created permanently and is not removed even if the underlying piece is later unpublished.

The scene-judgement override

A specific provision worth flagging because it is unusual among editorial publications. ContentLore maintains an explicit provision for scene-judgement overrides — situations where this desk's scene knowledge indicates that a publicly available source is incorrect.

For example: a public streaming metrics site may report that a particular creator is on a given server, when this desk knows through scene contact that the public source is out-of-date. In such cases, this desk's policy is to override the public source with the scene-verified information and flag the override in the piece itself. Readers see the override attribution (scene-knowledge override) so they can assess for themselves whether to trust the scene-verified claim over the public-record one.

Scene-judgement overrides are used only where the contradicting public source would mislead readers if reported without correction. They are not a vehicle for unsupported claims; every override is based on scene-verified fact that this desk is willing to stand behind under right-of-reply and correction procedures.

Harm reduction

ContentLore's coverage is designed not to cause avoidable harm to its subjects or to its readers. Three categories of harm are particularly attended to.

Harm to minors. This publication does not profile creators under eighteen. Where coverage of a UK scene development involves minors — for example, a younger creator's growth trajectory, or a server's handling of young community members — the coverage is framed to avoid amplifying content or dynamics that could harm young people. This applies regardless of whether the minor themselves is the subject of coverage or a peripheral figure.

Harm to wellbeing. Where coverage touches on creator mental health, scene-internal conflicts, or developments that a reader might find personally distressing, the framing is handled with restraint. This publication does not publish dramatised accounts of creator burnout, mental health crises, or personal struggles. Where a creator has been public about such matters, coverage respects that publicness without amplifying or sensationalising it.

Harm through inaccuracy. The corrections policy above is itself a harm-reduction mechanism. Inaccurate public claims about a person, server, or organisation damage the subject; the corrections mechanism exists to repair that damage promptly and visibly when it occurs.

AI disclosure

ContentLore uses AI tools in editorial workflow. Specifically, this publication uses large language models (including Claude, made by Anthropic) for drafting assistance, structural editing, and prose refinement. AI tools are not used for fact-finding, source verification, or decisions about what to publish; those remain human editorial judgements.

Every piece published on ContentLore is reviewed, verified, and approved by a human editor — Fats — before publication. Where AI-assisted drafts are used, they are subject to the same factual, ethical, and standards review as any other draft. AI does not change what this publication covers or how it covers it; it is a drafting tool applied within the editorial process, not a substitute for it.

AI is not used to generate fake quotes, invent sources, fabricate claims, or produce coverage that a human editor has not verified. Where AI has contributed substantively to a piece's structure or prose, that contribution is a routine part of the editorial process and does not require per-piece disclosure. Where AI has been used in an unusual way that might affect how a reader interprets the piece — for example, AI-generated imagery, AI-produced analysis of data, or AI-written sections that are meaningfully different in voice from the rest of the piece — that use is disclosed explicitly on the piece concerned.

What this document is not

Three clarifications to prevent this page from being misread.

It is not a legal policy. Terms and conditions, privacy policy, cookie policy, and similar legal pages sit separately from this editorial standards document. This page is about editorial practice; legal pages are about user contract.

It is not static. Editorial standards evolve as the publication grows and as the scene itself changes. Updates to this document are versioned (this is version 1.0, as of 19 April 2026) and logged in the Ledger. Historical versions remain accessible on request.

It is not a disclaimer. Publishing editorial standards is not a legal shield. A piece of coverage that fails to meet these standards is subject to correction and, where the failure is material, to retraction. Publishing this document increases this publication's obligations rather than reducing them.
