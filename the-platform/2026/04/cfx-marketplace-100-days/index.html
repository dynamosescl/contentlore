Cfx Marketplace at 100 days.

On 12 January 2026, Rockstar and Cfx.re launched the Cfx Marketplace — the first time in the platform's decade-long existence that paid mods have lived on a Rockstar-curated storefront. One hundred days in, what eighteen launch creators, £288 bundles (USD $389), and Tebex's quiet backbone reveal about the commercial architecture Rockstar is building.

By Fats 9 min read Issue 02

The easiest piece to write about the Cfx Marketplace is the outraged one. Rockstar, the company that spent a decade describing FiveM as an unauthorised multiplayer server, now runs an official storefront on which mods sell for up to three hundred and ninety dollars. The community-built ecosystem has a merchant of record. The ironies are ready-made and the commentary has already been written — by TechRadar, by PC Gamer, by fifty gaming blogs. That is not the piece this desk is going to write. The more useful question, one hundred days in, is what the Marketplace actually is underneath the irony — and the answer is more interesting than the outrage.

The Cfx Marketplace, launched 12 January 2026, is three things at once. It is a curated storefront on which approved creators sell mods to server operators. It is a revenue-share arrangement between Rockstar, Cfx.re, and the creators themselves. And — this is the quietest of the three but the most structurally significant — it is a Tebex-powered commercial stack, with Tebex listed as merchant of record at the footer of every page. Each of those three descriptions matters for different reasons. Together they describe a substantially new piece of the UK FiveM infrastructure, not a cosmetic update to an existing one.

For UK readers the interesting name on that list is London Studios. London Studios has been one of the most visible UK FiveM asset shops for years — an operation selling map packs, vehicle models, and scripts into the global FiveM market. Its inclusion on the launch roster means that, of the eighteen creators Rockstar hand-picked to legitimise first, one of them is a UK business. UK money is now flowing through the Rockstar-Cfx revenue split every time London Studios makes a sale on the Marketplace. That is not a dramatic observation. It is a structural one. The UK modding economy has a new commercial connection to Rockstar that it did not have four months ago.

The other names on the list tell a secondary story. This is a roster of high-end scripters and asset creators — Loaf Scripts, rcore, Codesign, LB Phone — not a roster of experimental hobbyists. Rockstar has chosen the professional tier of the modding ecosystem as the Marketplace's opening act. The message that sends to the community is: this is where serious mod developers sell serious work at serious prices. It is not a bazaar, and it is not a bedroom-coder incubator.

What £288 actually means

A note on pricing. Cfx lists Marketplace prices in US dollars. Sterling figures in this piece are converted at approximately GBP/USD 1.35 for UK reader context; the exact amount charged to a UK card will vary with FX rates at the time of billing. Cfx's USD list price is shown alongside the sterling figure where relevant.

The most-reported number from the Marketplace launch is the three-hundred-and-eighty-nine-dollar creator-bundle price ceiling. Most reporting frames this as shocking. It is worth sitting with the number for a moment because the framing matters more than the headline.

The highest-priced single item documented at launch was the MRPD, Hospital, and Mechanic map bundle at £149 (USD $201.29). The £288 ceiling (USD $389) applies to creator bundles — multi-product packages from a single creator offering their full catalogue at a discount to the sum of individual purchases. These are not consumer-facing prices. They are operator prices, targeted at server owners who need the content as commercial infrastructure for a paying community.

That distinction matters because the FiveM commercial picture is not a game being priced at £288. It is a business tool being priced at £288. The closest comparator is not buying a video game; it is buying a Shopify theme pack, or a WordPress plugin bundle, or an enterprise licence for a commercial Lua library. Server operators are running businesses — whitelisted RP communities with paying subscribers — and the Marketplace is tooling those businesses. Two hundred and eighty-eight dollars is defensible in that context. It only reads as outrageous if you apply consumer pricing logic to a business-to-business transaction.

Whether the community accepts that framing is a different question. The Marketplace's reception on the Cfx forum and in community Discords has been noticeably split. Server operators running as businesses tend to welcome a legitimate commercial channel. Creators hoping to start out tend to find the barriers high. Players encountering the Marketplace through coverage written for gaming audiences tend to read it through consumer framing and react accordingly. The Marketplace is, in that sense, positioned awkwardly — commercially coherent, culturally contested.

Tebex is the whole story

The detail this desk keeps returning to — and the one mainstream coverage has almost universally missed — is that the Cfx Marketplace runs on Tebex. Scroll to the footer of marketplace.cfx.re and the line reads: Merchant: Tebex. Rockstar Games, Inc. © 2016-2026, FiveM is a copyright and registered trademark of Take-Two Interactive Software, Inc. That single line describes the commercial architecture of the entire FiveM ecosystem in 2026.

Tebex is not a vendor Rockstar uses. Tebex is the infrastructure Rockstar's new commercial layer sits on.

Tebex has been FiveM's exclusive monetisation partner for years. Individual UK servers process their community subscriptions, priority queue passes, and cosmetic sales through Tebex stores. That arrangement was already structurally significant for the scene. The Marketplace launch makes it substantially more so. Now Rockstar's first-party storefront, the one with eighteen hand-picked professional creators and prices reaching £288, also runs on Tebex. Rockstar did not build its own commercial stack. It put its storefront on top of the one that was already there.

For UK server operators this matters in at least two practical ways. It matters because any policy change Tebex applies — to payment flows, to refund rules, to permitted product categories, to pricing constraints — now affects the Rockstar Marketplace as well as every individual server store. Tebex's leverage in the ecosystem has grown, not shrunk, with the Marketplace launch. It also matters because server operators who have been running their Tebex stores loosely — with product categories or pricing structures that sit in grey-area relationship with the PLA — are now sharing a commercial platform with Rockstar's own storefront. The PLA enforcement posture is likely to tighten.

The Tebex Audit, this desk's inaugural systematic survey of UK server Tebex stores, lands in early May. It will cover exactly these questions for every UK server with a public store: what is sold, at what price, under what category, with what PLA compliance posture. The Marketplace's launch is part of why the audit is now overdue. A commercial layer with Rockstar's name on it changes the stakes for everyone else on the same platform.

The revenue split

Rockstar has not publicly disclosed the exact revenue-share percentages between creator, Cfx, and Rockstar for Marketplace sales. Community reporting and creator comments point to a meaningful Rockstar-and-Cfx take, but the specific split remains commercial-in-confidence. That opacity is editorially unsatisfying and worth noting explicitly.

What we can infer from creator pricing behaviour is that the split is non-trivial. Several launch creators raised prices on the Marketplace relative to their pre-Marketplace Tebex store prices, suggesting the new channel's costs have been passed on to operators. That is a reasonable commercial response; it is also a useful tell. When creators raise prices to maintain margins under a new arrangement, the new arrangement is taking a share that matters. The exact percentages will emerge over time as creators become more transparent about margins or as Rockstar is compelled to disclose for regulatory or community reasons. For now, the correct reporting posture is that the split is material and undisclosed, not that it is a specific number.

What the Marketplace is not

Three clarifications worth making, because each one is routinely confused in general coverage.

It is not a mod-install-in-one-click system for players. The Marketplace sells to server operators, who then deploy the purchased content on their servers. A player on a given FiveM server experiences Marketplace content as part of that server's offering, not as a mod they personally bought. The commercial relationship runs through the operator, not the player.

It is not a replacement for the existing FiveM ecosystem. Thousands of free scripts, open-source resources, and community releases continue to exist alongside the Marketplace. Many of the most widely used frameworks — ESX, QBCore, QBOX — remain free. The Marketplace is a new commercial tier, not a displacement of the free one.

It is not currently open to all creators. The launch roster of eighteen expanded modestly in the first hundred days through an application-based process. Rockstar and Cfx describe the roll-out as phased and curated. That curation decision is itself editorially significant: Rockstar is choosing which mod shops to legitimise, and by implication, which ones it is not. The criteria for admission are opaque.

Why this matters for UK servers

Reading the Marketplace launch as a UK server operator, three operational implications fall out of the analysis above. Each is worth sitting with before it becomes a migration-season decision.

Commercial costs on assets are rising. Server operators who were used to free or cheap community scripts should plan for a Marketplace-tier pricing structure to spread across the creator ecosystem over the next twelve months. Creators who were selling at community prices on Discord are being pulled toward Marketplace-comparable prices on their own Tebex stores. The cost line for custom content is structurally rising. Budgeting needs to adjust.

PLA enforcement is tightening. Rockstar now has a commercial stake in FiveM's PLA being respected, because its own storefront sits on the same commercial rails as the servers the PLA governs. Servers with Tebex stores running in grey-area relationship with the PLA's "no pay to win" provisions should expect a less forgiving environment going forward. This desk's Tebex Audit will cover this ground systematically in early May.

The legitimate commercial channel is worth engaging with. For UK server operators who want to sell their own original work, the Marketplace's application route is now open. London Studios is on the list. There is no structural reason a UK creator with professional-quality work cannot apply. The cultural question — whether joining a Rockstar-monetised ecosystem fits an individual creator's values — is a personal one. But the commercial door is open.

What to watch for next

The Marketplace has been live for one hundred days. The next hundred will tell us more. Three specific developments are worth tracking. The roster's expansion rate — whether Rockstar keeps curation tight or opens the doors meaningfully. The pricing trajectory — whether the high-end prices hold or come down under market pressure. The emergence of regional representation — whether London Studios remains the only UK creator on the roster, or whether Rockstar explicitly recruits additional geographies to balance what is currently a US-and-Europe-heavy list.

The Platform will cover the Marketplace as a running beat. The next scheduled deep dive in this area will mark the Marketplace's six-month anniversary in July, pairing with the alt:V shutdown coverage. Between now and then, expect dispatches on any significant roster changes, pricing shifts, or policy announcements. The Tebex Audit in May will pick up the commercial-architecture thread across every UK server's store, Marketplace or otherwise.
