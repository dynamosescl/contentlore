FiveM for GTA V Enhanced, translated.

Cfx.re's first development update in months landed on 18 March. It is the most consequential document for UK GTA RP this year. Here is what server operators need to read between the lines before migration season begins.

By Fats 8 min read Issue 01

There is a paragraph near the top of Cfx.re's March development update that every UK server operator should read twice. It begins: "Our goal with this release is not to simply bring the existing FiveM experience over to GTAV Enhanced." The sentence is the kind of corporate prose that reads like throat-clearing. It is not. It is the entire thesis of what is coming next, and it is the reason this is not a simple version bump to plan around. Cfx is rebuilding the platform. The platform is the thing UK servers run on. The rebuild is not optional.

What follows is a careful read of the 18 March 2026 Cfx announcement, translated into the decisions UK server operators are going to need to make over the next six to nine months. Primary source is linked at the end. Where Cfx has left something unsaid, this piece says so. Where the scene's reading of the document differs from the official framing, this piece says that too.

What Cfx actually announced

On 18 March 2026, Cfx.re published the first of what it called a blog series covering FiveM for GTAV Enhanced — the rebuilt FiveM that will run on Rockstar's next-generation PC release of GTA V. The core claims in the post, summarised without the marketing polish:

  • FiveM is being rebuilt, not ported. The team is rewriting core components rather than lifting the existing platform onto a new game version. Cfx frames this as an opportunity. It is also a decision that carries real migration cost.
  • OneSync has been overhauled. OneSync is the networking engine that lets FiveM servers scale past GTA Online's base-game player cap. The new version claims reduced bandwidth, CPU, and memory consumption at equivalent player counts.
  • All Enhanced servers will run high-performance sync only. The older, lower-capacity sync modes are being dropped entirely. Servers relying on them today are going to have to adjust.
  • Existing scripts are backward-compatible. Resources written in Lua, JavaScript, or C# continue to work on the rebuilt platform. This matters more than it might sound; the UK scene runs on a decade's worth of community-built scripts. A related technical change: the Mono runtime that powers C# scripts is being replaced under the hood, which C# scripters on UK servers will want to follow as details emerge.
  • 3D assets need conversion via Alchemist. The tool Cfx released in November 2025 for exactly this purpose. Servers with extensive custom map content will be leaning on it heavily.
  • Some of the FiveM codebase is going closed-source. Cfx says this is "tied to the deeper integration with the base game" and promises more detail "over time." The explanation is high-level.
  • Platinum Element Club is still required for 2,048-player servers. None of the subscription tiers change. What the tiers buy, changes.
  • Release is "in the coming months" via early access. No date. Server owners will get first access for feedback and testing.

That is the announcement at face value. The interesting material is in the gaps.

The three decisions UK servers now have to make

Reading the Cfx post as an operator rather than a developer, three real decisions come out of it. They are not technically complex. They are commercially and editorially consequential, and they land inside a compressed window.

Decision one · When to migrate

FiveM for Enhanced is launching via early access. Early access means the first servers that migrate get to find the bugs Cfx's internal testing missed. They also get to be seen doing it — early migration is a marketing signal to players that a server is run by competent operators with technical capacity. Late migration is the safer engineering choice but creates a period where a server is visibly stuck on Legacy while competitors have moved.

For UK servers, this decision is not symmetric across the pack. A whitelisted server running stable, serious RP with 150 dedicated players can afford to wait. A server trading on production polish and wanting to advertise Enhanced-tier performance needs to migrate fast. A server sitting on a framework that is itself mid-transition — any server that was considering QBOX, for example — has an opportunity to make both decisions in one step rather than two, which is a cleaner migration story for its community.

Timing guidance from this desk: the early access rollout is likely to land inside the summer months based on the "coming months" language and the broader Cfx development cadence. UK servers should have a migration plan drafted by end of May 2026, a test environment by June, and be ready to move into early access within two weeks of Cfx opening the door to non-invited operators. That is tight. It is achievable for serious servers and uncomfortable for everyone else.

Decision two · Asset inventory

The Alchemist tool exists to convert 3D assets from Legacy FiveM to Enhanced-compatible formats. It was released in November 2025 and Cfx partnered with the Sollumz Blender plugin shortly afterwards. For servers whose custom content is script-only, asset conversion is a non-event. For servers built around custom maps, vehicles, or extensive YMAP work — in the UK, that includes every serious server in the Scoreboard — asset migration is a defined piece of work with a real hour cost.

Cfx has said nothing concrete about escrowed assets yet. The forum responses on the 18 March thread include repeated requests from operators asking exactly this question. Escrow is the mechanism by which paid FiveM resources protect their source from casual theft; a lot of commercial scripts and assets are escrowed. If escrow does not survive the migration cleanly, servers that licensed paid content under the old model have a conversation with their vendors ahead of them. This is an unknown worth watching.

Practical step for UK operators: audit your server's asset inventory now. Tag every custom 3D asset, note its source, note whether it is escrowed, and note whether the original creator is still active and reachable. Assets whose creators have left the scene are assets that will not be re-released for Enhanced. Knowing what those are gives you three months to find replacements.

Decision three · The closed-source question

This is the announcement's most politically charged paragraph and the one that has received the least analytical coverage so far. Cfx has said that parts of the FiveM codebase will move from open to closed source in the Enhanced build. The framing is: "This change is tied to the deeper integration with the base game required to deliver a more stable and maintainable implementation."

That is not a full explanation. It is a statement about direction without a statement about scope. Which components are closing? Under what licence? With what audit rights for the community? None of this has been specified. The 18 March post acknowledges this explicitly: "We will share more detail over time about what this means in practice."

For most UK servers, the closed-source shift changes nothing operationally. Servers run what Cfx ships; they do not routinely read or modify the platform source. The shift matters anyway, for three reasons.

First, it changes FiveM's character. FiveM has been a source-available platform for its entire existence. That was part of the scene's identity — the reason contributors have written the community features that make FiveM what it is. Closing parts of the codebase shifts FiveM from community project to Rockstar product, and the community notices. The scene's reaction to this shift will shape Cfx's political room to make further changes.

Second, it changes what UK server operators can verify. If OneSync's implementation is closed, a server experiencing unexplained sync problems cannot read the code to understand what is happening. Trust in Cfx's technical claims becomes more important because operators cannot audit them directly.

Third, it changes the insurance case for choosing FiveM over alternatives. UK servers that survived the alt:V shutdown announcement did so partly on the reasoning that FiveM was community-owned and therefore harder to kill unilaterally. That case is now weaker. Not weak — Rockstar has made significant public investment in FiveM — but weaker. This matters for servers weighing long-term framework bets.

What Cfx did not say

Four omissions from the 18 March post are worth flagging, because they are the questions UK operators will want answered before they make migration decisions.

No pricing guidance. The Element Club tiers are unchanged at announcement, but the question of whether a tier unlocks the same features on Enhanced as on Legacy is not explicitly addressed for anything below the Platinum 2,048-slot bucket. A server currently on Aurum needs to know whether Aurum on Enhanced gets the same benefits or a revised package.

No migration toolkit. Alchemist exists for 3D assets. There is no equivalent announced for server configurations, Tebex integrations, or the dozen other pieces of operational tooling servers depend on. Cfx presumably has these plans but has not shared them.

No RedM timeline. RedM — the Red Dead Redemption 2 FiveM equivalent — is mentioned nowhere in the Enhanced post. RedM has a smaller UK footprint but a passionate one, and the silence reads as implicit: Enhanced for FiveM first, RedM gets its update later.

No commitment on legacy support duration. Cfx has not said how long Legacy FiveM will continue to be maintained after Enhanced launches. Servers that delay migration need to know whether they are staying on a platform that will receive bug fixes for eighteen months or six. The ambiguity favours early migration. Whether that is deliberate is a question for another piece.

The alt:V timing

Any UK operator reading this who also ran an alt:V server is reading it inside a different calendar. alt:V shuts down on 6 July 2026. Those servers are migrating regardless; the question is what they are migrating to. FiveM Legacy is the default answer and has been for six months. FiveM for Enhanced — coming in the same window as alt:V's final month — is a more attractive destination if the timing lines up.

For an alt:V server that has already decided FiveM is its destination, the Enhanced announcement means the migration calendar just got harder. Ship to Legacy now and face a second migration within months. Wait for Enhanced and face the alt:V shutdown with no operational home. Split the move across the transition — ship to Legacy, then to Enhanced — and pay migration costs twice. None of these are good. Each has a real cost. The right answer depends on a server's engineering capacity and its community's patience.

The NoPixel V parallel track

One further transition thread matters here, and it is the one most easily mistaken for a sub-plot of the Enhanced story when it is actually a separate structure. NoPixel V — announced in September 2025 as a Rockstar-backed reboot of the world's largest GTA RP server — is shipping on the Rockstar Games Launcher and other PC platforms, not via the standard FiveM server list. That distinction matters. NoPixel V is not a FiveM server being migrated to Enhanced. It is a Rockstar co-produced title running on Rockstar's own distribution rails, with Cfx tooling underneath.

For UK operators, this is worth reading as a signal about where Rockstar's strategic ambition for RP actually sits. FiveM for Enhanced is the platform that will carry the existing ecosystem forward. NoPixel V is the model for what a first-party Rockstar RP product looks like — gated distribution, integrated launcher, and a commercial relationship closer to a publisher-developer deal than a community platform. Neither displaces the other; they are parallel tracks, and any UK server operator planning beyond the Enhanced migration needs to think about both.

The editorial implication is narrower. Most UK servers will continue on FiveM for Enhanced. A handful of the largest or best-connected might, in time, pursue something closer to the NoPixel V arrangement with Rockstar. For now, NoPixel V matters mostly because it confirms Rockstar's willingness to build alternative distribution paths for RP content, which shapes expectations for what FiveM's long-term role inside Rockstar's strategy actually is.

The one thing you should not do

If you run a UK FiveM server in any capacity and one member of your staff is reading this piece on a PC they use for testing, there is one concrete piece of operational advice worth stating bluntly: do not upgrade your GTA V installation to Enhanced yet. The Enhanced build is a separate version of the base game. Current FiveM runs on Legacy only. A staff machine that upgrades early loses FiveM access until Enhanced support officially ships, which can interrupt test environments and staff testing cycles mid-sprint. The correct move is to maintain Legacy on testing machines until Cfx confirms Enhanced is ready for community use. This applies to players too, but the operational impact is sharpest on staff who are running test servers.

What to watch for next

Cfx described its 18 March post as the first in a blog series. The second post is overdue. When it lands, the questions this desk is reading it for are: escrow's future, pricing clarity on sub-Platinum tiers, the specific components moving closed-source, and any indication of a concrete early-access date. A post that addresses all four would be a confidence-building signal. A post that addresses none of them would tell UK operators they need to plan for Enhanced without Cfx-provided answers on the questions that matter.
