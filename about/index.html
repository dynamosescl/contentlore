The UK streaming desk.

ContentLore is a publication about the UK streaming scene — the creators, the communities, the platforms they stream on, and the servers that matter. This page explains what that means, who runs it, and why it exists as a standalone publication rather than one more voice in the scene's existing channels.

British streaming is one of the fastest-moving creative industries in the country and one of the least systematically covered. Thousands of creators work full-time or substantially so across Twitch, Kick, YouTube and TikTok. Millions of viewers watch them. Hundreds of millions of pounds move through the ecosystem — subscription revenue, brand deals, platform payouts, roleplay server monetisation, merchandise, live events. None of this has a serious dedicated publication covering it from a UK perspective. Mainstream gaming and streaming press is overwhelmingly US-centric. UK scene coverage happens mostly inside the scene — Discord channels, Twitter threads, creator podcasts — which is valuable but incomplete. There is no equivalent to what The Athletic did for football, or The Information did for tech. ContentLore exists to fill that gap.

What this publication is

ContentLore is an editorial publication covering the UK streaming scene across four platforms — Twitch, Kick, YouTube and TikTok — and the ecosystem infrastructure around them, most significantly the FiveM roleplay server scene that has become central to British streaming. It publishes intelligence, analysis, and commentary at several cadences: daily signals on the homepage ticker, weekly coverage across the beats, deep dives on the pieces that warrant longer treatment, and quarterly set pieces like the Tebex Audit.

The publication is structured around five beats, each covering a distinct part of the scene:

GTA RP covers the British roleplay server scene. The Scoreboard, the long-term server leaderboard, sits at the centre of this beat. Expect detailed tracking of server-level movements, player-base shifts, scene-wide narrative arcs, and the individual creators who shape RP culture in the UK.

The Platform covers FiveM and the infrastructure that sits underneath every UK RP server — Cfx.re, the Element Club tiers, the frameworks, the Tebex monetisation layer, the Platform License Agreement, and Rockstar's evolving relationship with the modding scene. The audit series and long-form Platform pieces sit here.

Rising covers creators on their way up — the names the scene will be talking about in twelve months, documented while their trajectory is still ascending. This is discovery-oriented coverage: who is worth watching, what they are doing differently, and what their growth curve looks like.

Signals is the weekly and monthly intelligence brief. Rolled-up coverage of scene-wide patterns — platform moves, creator migrations, commercial developments, scene sentiment shifts — published on a regular schedule so readers can see the shape of the week without having to track every individual event.

The Ledger is the transparency and corrections log. Every correction, every updated fact, every editorial decision worth disclosing is logged there permanently. This exists as a public accountability mechanism and is documented further in this publication's editorial standards.

Positioning

Three things make ContentLore distinct from existing UK streaming coverage.

It is UK-first. Every piece is written for a UK reader, with UK context, UK pricing in sterling, UK regulatory frameworks where relevant, and UK servers and creators as the primary subjects. When coverage touches US or European developments — as the Cfx Marketplace and alt:V shutdown coverage does — those are covered from a UK perspective and connected back to UK implications. This is not a UK section of a global publication; it is a UK publication that occasionally looks outward.

It is written from inside the scene. ContentLore is not written by distant journalists covering streaming as one of many beats. It is written by someone who streams, who knows the operators, who has been in the Discord channels for years, and who understands why a given server rivalry matters even when it does not make sense from outside. The cost of that proximity is careful conflict-of-interest management, documented in the editorial standards. The benefit is coverage that actually understands the things it is writing about.

It is systematic, not reactive. Most scene coverage reacts to events as they happen — drama, news, controversy. ContentLore covers those where they matter, but the central commitment is to systematic documentation: the Scoreboard updates on a permanent cadence, the Tebex Audit runs quarterly, the reference pages are maintained indefinitely. The point is to build an archive of scene intelligence that survives past whatever is trending this week, so readers have a permanent reference point for what the UK scene actually looks like over time.

Who runs it

ContentLore is founded and edited by Fats, who streams on Kick under the handle Dynamoses. Fats is a UK streamer and content creator with longstanding presence in British streaming communities, with a professional background in data and insights work that directly shapes how this publication approaches its coverage — systematic, quantitative where the data supports it, evidenced, and focused on what patterns reveal over time rather than what a single event means in isolation.

Editorial decisions — what gets covered, how it gets framed, which sources are used, what corrections are made — are Fats' responsibility. Where contributors, researchers, or scene sources inform specific pieces, they are credited by name or acknowledged anonymously where they have requested confidentiality. The Ledger records when this happens.

A public masthead listing any contributors or advisory relationships will be maintained on this page as the publication grows. Where any named individual has an editorial relationship with ContentLore, that relationship will be disclosed here and on pieces where it is relevant.

What this publication does not cover

Four scope exclusions worth stating explicitly because each of them is a regular reader question.

Non-UK creators, unless the coverage has UK implications. American, European, Australian and other non-UK streaming scenes exist and have excellent dedicated coverage elsewhere. ContentLore covers them only where a development — a platform policy change, a major creator move, a commercial shift — has direct consequences for UK creators or UK viewers. The alt:V shutdown is covered because it affects UK FiveM servers. NoPixel's internal dynamics are covered where they shape the UK RP landscape. A routine American creator drama, by contrast, is not in scope.

Breaking drama as the primary story. Scene drama exists; it is often genuinely newsworthy. But ContentLore's editorial position is that drama is usually only interesting as a symptom of deeper structural things — commercial pressures, platform incentives, scene hierarchies — and the deeper story is what this publication tries to cover. When a specific piece of drama genuinely reveals something structural, it gets covered. When it is just drama, it does not.

Minors or child-adjacent content. ContentLore does not cover creators under eighteen, does not profile child audiences, and is particularly careful about coverage of any scene dynamics involving minors. This is documented in more detail in the editorial standards.

Paid promotion of any kind. ContentLore does not accept sponsored coverage, advertising on the site, affiliate commissions from covered entities, paid-for features, or any other arrangement where money flows from covered subjects to this publication. This is not a discretionary editorial decision; it is a structural commitment. Further detail is in the editorial standards.

Why "ContentLore"

The name reflects what this publication is trying to do: document the lore of the scene over time. In streaming culture, lore is a term for the accumulated narrative history of a character, a server, a rivalry, or a community — the backstory that makes a given moment meaningful. ContentLore is an attempt to document the lore of the wider UK streaming scene with the seriousness that lore-level coverage deserves: systematic, evidenced, and built to survive past the moment.

Full contact channels, including how to send scene tips, how to request right-of-reply on coverage, and how to submit corrections, are documented on the Contact page. For general enquiries, the same contact routes apply.
